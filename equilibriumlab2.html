Part 2 · The Critical Distinction

Same equation.
Different universe.

Equilibrium says F₁ = −F₂ — two forces on one body, balancing each other.
Interaction says F_AB = −F_BA — two forces on two different bodies, always paired.

The math is identical. The physics is completely different. Your mission in Part 2: master this distinction so completely that no trick question can fool you.

E2 · The Core Distinction

Equilibrium vs Interaction

Both relationships use the same vector equation. The critical difference lies in which bodies the forces act upon.

Criterion ⚖ Equilibrium Condition 🔄 Principle of Interaction
EquationF₁ = −F₂F_AB = −F_BA
Bodies involvedONE body — both forces act on the same objectTWO bodies — each force acts on a different object
What they produceZero net force → body stays at rest or constant velocityAlways exist in pairs — you cannot have one without the other
Same object?✓ Yes — both forces on same body✗ No — forces on DIFFERENT bodies
Can they cancel?✓ Yes — they cancel → equilibrium✗ No — they act on different bodies so they CANNOT cancel each other
Classic exampleBook on table: Weight ↓ and Normal ↑ both act on the bookBook on table: Force(table→book) ↑ acts on book · Force(book→table) ↓ acts on table
Key test questionAre both forces on the same object?Do the forces act on different objects and form a pair?
⚠ Common Misconception
Many students look at a book on a table and say: "Normal force N and Weight W are an interaction pair because they are equal and opposite."

This is WRONG. N and W are both on the book — so they form an equilibrium pair.

The true interaction pair is: F(table→book) ↑ [acts on book] and F(book→table) ↓ [acts on table].
✓ The Two-Question Test
Given any two forces, ask:

Question 1: Are both forces acting on the same body?
→ If YES → could be an equilibrium pair

Question 2: Do the forces act on different bodies and always exist together?
→ If YES → it is an interaction pair

Question 2 · Application
A lamp hangs from a ceiling by a wire. The tension T in the wire acts upward on the lamp. The weight W acts downward on the lamp. These form:
AAn interaction pair — T is the reaction to W
BAn equilibrium pair — both act on the lamp, making it stationary
CNeither — they are not related
DAn interaction pair — equal and opposite forces always form interaction pairs
Question 3 · The Classic Trap
A student says: "The weight W of a book and the normal force N of the table on the book are an interaction pair because N = −W."
What is wrong with this statement?
AN ≠ W in magnitude so they cannot be paired
BBoth N and W act on the book — they are an equilibrium pair. The true interaction partner of N is F(book→table) which acts on the TABLE
CInteraction pairs must involve only contact forces
DN and W are not equal and opposite
Question 4 · Deduction
A ball of weight W = 8 N floats on water. The buoyancy force F_b acts upward. Using the equilibrium condition, deduce the magnitude and direction of F_b, and name the interaction partner of F_b.
AF_b = 8 N downward · interaction partner = W
BF_b = 8 N upward · interaction partner = force ball exerts on water downward (8 N)
CF_b = 4 N upward · it balances half of W
DF_b = 8 N upward · interaction partner = W (weight of ball)
Question 5 · True or False
"An interaction pair can produce equilibrium of a single body if one force is removed."
ATrue — removing one force leaves the other to balance
BFalse — interaction pairs act on different bodies so they can never produce equilibrium of one body. Each body needs its OWN forces to balance.
