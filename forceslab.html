E3 · Forces & Their Properties

Understanding Forces

A force is a push or pull. Every force has four characteristics that completely describe it.

Four Characteristics of Every Force

CharacteristicWhat it meansOn a diagramExample
Point of ApplicationThe exact point where the force acts on the bodyThe tail of the force arrowContact force → contact point. Weight → centre of gravity G
Line of ActionThe straight line along which the force acts (extended in both directions)The line containing the arrowWeight acts along a vertical line through G
DirectionWhich way along the line of action the force pushes/pullsThe arrowheadWeight acts downward. Normal reaction acts upward
MagnitudeHow strong the force isThe length of the arrow (to scale)Weight = mg (in Newtons)

Force Classification

Contact Forces
Require direct physical contact between the two objects.
⬆ Normal reaction of ground/table on object
↙ Friction force on sliding/stationary object
⬆ Buoyant force by liquid on floating object
↑ Tension in a string/spring on an object
Point of application: at the contact point
Forces at a Distance
Act across empty space without any physical contact.
⬇ Weight (gravitational force) on all objects
↔ Electric force between charged objects
→ Magnetic force by magnet on iron/nail
Point of application: at centre of gravity G
How to Name a Force

Always name a force using: [Type of force] by [source] on [object]

✓ Weight of the box
✓ Normal reaction of table on box
✓ Magnetic force by magnet on nail
✓ Electric force by charge A on charge B
✓ Buoyant force by mercury on ball
✓ Tension of spring on mass

Apply Your Knowledge

Six scenarios. Name the forces, classify them, identify the correct point of application.

Problem 01 · Force Identification
🏋️ A metal ball is suspended from the free end of a vertical spring. The ball is at rest.
How many forces act on the ball? Select the complete and correct list:
AWeight only — gravity pulls it down
BWeight of ball + Tension of spring on ball
CWeight + Tension + Normal reaction
DWeight + Tension + Friction
Problem 02 · Point of Application
📦 A wooden box rests on a rough inclined plane. Points are labelled: G (centre of box), A (bottom surface of box), B (side surface of box).
Match each force to its correct point of application:
AWeight at G · Normal reaction at A · Friction at A
BWeight at A · Normal reaction at G · Friction at B
CAll three forces applied at G (centre of gravity)
DWeight at G · Normal reaction at B · Friction at A
⚠ Misconception Trap
Problem 03 · True or False
🔮 Two positively charged spheres A and B repel each other. They are not touching.
A student says: "The electric force by A on B should be drawn from the surface of B closest to A." Is this correct?
ATrue — the force acts at the surface where contact would occur
BFalse — electric force is a distance force, so it must be drawn from the centre of gravity G of sphere B
Problem 04 · Force Classification
⚽ A ball floats on the surface of liquid mercury. Three forces act on it.
Classify each force correctly:
AWeight = contact · Buoyant force = contact · Normal = distance
BWeight = distance · Buoyant force = contact · Normal reaction = contact
CAll three are contact forces — they all push or pull the ball
DWeight = distance · Buoyant force = distance · Normal = contact
Problem 05 · Direction of Forces
🧲 An iron nail rests on a table. A magnet is held above the nail. The nail does not move yet.
What are the correct directions of all forces acting on the nail?
AWeight downward · Normal reaction upward · Magnetic force upward
BWeight downward · Normal reaction downward · Magnetic force upward
CWeight downward · Magnetic force upward only (no normal reaction — nail may lift)
DAll forces act downward toward Earth
