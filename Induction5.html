Exercise I Electromagnetic Induction — The Alternator Principle (10 pts)

A flat rectangular coil made of N = 100 turns, each having an area A = 40 cm2, rotates at a constant angular velocity ω = 50π rad·s−1 about an axis (Δ) located in its plane. The coil is immersed in a uniform magnetic field B⃗ of magnitude B = 0.50 T, perpendicular to the axis of rotation.

The orientation of the coil is defined by a unit normal vector n⃗. At time t = 0, the normal vector n⃗ is perfectly aligned and parallel to the magnetic field B⃗ (i.e., the angle between them is zero).

Given Data:
N = 100 turns A = 40 cm² = 4.0 × 10−3 m² B = 0.50 T ω = 50π rad·s−1 Coil resistance: rc = 0 Ω (ideal)
Part A — Magnetic Flux & Induced EMF
1. Let θ be the angle between the magnetic field B⃗ and the normal vector n⃗. Determine the expression of θ as a function of time t, knowing that θ = 0 at t = 0. (1 pt)
2. Show that the algebraic magnetic flux through the coil is given by the expression: Φ(t) = 0.20 \cos(50\pi t) (in Webers). (1.5 pts)
3. Apply Faraday's Law to derive the expression of the induced electromotive force e(t) across the coil. Deduce its maximum (peak) value Emax. (2 pts)
4. Calculate the period T and the frequency f of the alternating voltage produced by this generator. (1.5 pts)
Part B — Power in a Resistive Circuit

The rotating coil is now connected across a pure resistor of resistance R = 10 Ω.

5. Determine the expression of the instantaneous current i(t) flowing through the resistor. (1.5 pts)
6. Calculate the effective (RMS) value of the current, Ieff. (1 pt)
7. Calculate the average electrical power Pav consumed by the resistor. (1.5 pts)
Exercise II Self-Induction — Phenomenon & Steady State (10 pts)

To demonstrate the phenomenon of self-induction, we construct the circuit shown in Figure 2. It consists of an ideal DC voltage source of electromotive force E = 12.0 V, a main switch K, and two parallel branches:

  • Branch 1: Contains a resistor R1 in series with an incandescent lamp L1. The total equivalent resistance of this branch is Req1 = 6.0 Ω.
  • Branch 2: Contains a coil of self-inductance L = 0.80 H and internal resistance r = 2.0 Ω, in series with a second identical lamp L2. The total equivalent resistance of this branch (including the coil's resistance) is Req2 = 6.0 Ω.

Initially, switch K is open and no current flows in the circuit.

Part A — The Transient Phenomenon (Closing the circuit)
1. At t = 0, switch K is closed. Describe what is observed visually concerning the lighting of lamps L1 and L2. (1.5 pts)
2. Using Lenz’s Law and the concept of self-induced electromotive force e = -L \frac{di}{dt}, explain the physical cause of the delay observed in Branch 2. (2.5 pts)
Part B — The Steady State (Permanent Regime)

After a long time, the currents in both branches reach a constant (steady) value.

3. In this steady state, what is the value of the self-induced electromotive force e across the coil? Justify your answer. (1.5 pts)
4. Calculate the steady current I1 flowing through Branch 1 and the steady current I2 flowing through Branch 2. Will the two lamps eventually glow with the same brightness? (2 pts)
5. Calculate the magnetic energy Wm stored in the coil during this steady state. (1 pt)
Part C — Average Induced EMF (Opening the circuit)
6. The switch K is suddenly opened. The current in the coil drops from its steady value I2 down to zero in a very short time interval Δt = 2.0 ms.

Calculate the average self-induced electromotive force e_{av} = -L \frac{\Delta i}{\Delta t} generated by the coil during this interruption. What physical phenomenon can this high voltage cause at the terminals of the switch? (1.5 pts)

Exercise II — Self-Induction Phenomenon (10 pts)
QExpected Answer / Key PointsFormula / ValueMarks
1 Upon closing switch K, lamp L₁ lights up instantly, reaching its maximum brightness immediately. Lamp L₂ lights up gradually, taking a noticeable amount of time to reach its maximum brightness. L₁ instant, L₂ delayed 1.5
2 When K is closed, the current i in the coil attempts to increase (di/dt > 0). According to Faraday's and Lenz's laws, the coil creates a self-induced emf (e = -L di/dt) that acts as a "back emf" opposing the very increase in current that produces it. This magnetic inertia delays the establishment of the current in Branch 2. Coil opposes current increase 2.5
3 In the steady state, the current is constant. Therefore, its derivative is zero (di/dt = 0). The self-induced emf e = -L(di/dt) becomes exactly 0 V. The coil behaves purely as an ohmic conductor of resistance r. di/dt = 0 ⇒ e = 0 V 1.5
4 The branches are in parallel with the ideal source E.
Branch 1: I₁ = E / Req1 = 12.0 / 6.0 = 2.0 A.
Branch 2: I₂ = E / Req2 = 12.0 / 6.0 = 2.0 A.
Since both identical lamps are traversed by the exact same steady current (2.0 A), they will eventually glow with identical brightness.		 I₁ = 2.0 A
I₂ = 2.0 A		 2.0
5 Magnetic energy stored: Wm = ½ L I₂².
Wm = 0.5 × 0.80 × (2.0)² = 0.40 × 4.0 = 1.6 J.		 Wm = 1.6 J 1.0
6 Variation of current: Δi = ifinal - iinitial = 0 - 2.0 = -2.0 A.
eav = -L (Δi / Δt) = -0.80 × (-2.0 / 0.002) = -0.80 × (-1000) = +800 V.
This extremely high voltage appears across the open switch contacts, ionizing the air and creating an electrical spark (inductive kickback).		 eav = +800 V
