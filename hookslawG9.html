E3 · Explain

Deriving Hooke's Law

Your data showed a constant ratio T/Δx. Let's formalise this discovery step by step and derive the complete law — including what the graph tells us.

Step 1 · The Observation

From your experiment: as the applied force T (tension in the spring) increases, the elongation Δx increases proportionally. Double the force → double the elongation.

T  ∝  Δx
Step 2 · Introduce the Constant

To convert proportionality into an equation, we introduce a constant k — the spring constant (or force constant). It measures how stiff the spring is.

T = k · Δx

Spring constant (N/m)

Step 3 · The Graph

Plot T (vertical axis) against Δx (horizontal axis).

  • ✓ Straight line through the origin
  • ✓ Positive gradient
  • ✓ Gradient = k (spring constant)
  • ✓ Greater k → steeper line → stiffer spring
gradient = ΔT / Δ(Δx) = k
T vs Δx — three springs compared
Step 4 · The Elastic Limit ⚠

Hooke's Law only holds within the elastic limit. If you stretch a spring beyond this point, the spring is permanently deformed — it does not return to its original length. The T vs Δx graph becomes curved beyond this point. The spring has left its elastic region and entered its plastic region.

Elastic region
Spring returns to original length · T = k·Δx applies · Graph is linear
Plastic region
Permanent deformation · Hooke's Law breaks down · Graph curves
Step 5 · Sign Convention (Compression vs Elongation)

Hooke's Law also applies when a spring is compressed. The restoring force always acts opposite to the displacement: if you stretch the spring downward (Δx positive), the tension pulls upward (T positive). If you compress upward (Δx negative), the restoring force pushes downward (T negative). This is the restoring force principle.

T = k · Δx   works for both elongation (+Δx) and compression (−Δx)

E4 · Elaborate

Hooke's Law in the Real World

Springs are everywhere. Every one of these follows T = k·Δx — within the elastic limit.

🚗
Car Suspension
Each wheel sits on a spring. When you hit a pothole, the spring compresses (−Δx), absorbing the shock so passengers don't feel it. Lebanon's roads make this very relevant!
T = k · Δx  →  k must be chosen for car's mass
🏋️
Weighing Scale
A spring scale works exactly on Hooke's Law. The elongation Δx is proportional to weight (force). The scale just reads Δx and converts it to mass. Every market stall uses this.
W = k · Δx  →  m = kΔx / g
🏹
Archery Bow
A bow stores elastic potential energy as you pull the string back (elongation Δx). The stiffer the bow (higher k), the more force required — and the more energy released when fired.
Eelastic = ½ k (Δx)²
❤️
Medical Stents
Tiny metal springs (stents) are inserted into blocked arteries. Their spring constant is designed so they hold arteries open with exactly the right force — not crushing the vessel.
Precise k selection = life-saving medicine
🎸
Guitar Strings
A guitar string behaves like a spring under tension. Tightening the tuning peg increases the spring tension T, which raises the vibration frequency and the musical pitch.
Higher T → higher frequency → higher note
🌉
Suspension Bridges
The main cables of suspension bridges stretch elastically under load. Engineers calculate Δx under maximum traffic load to ensure the bridge stays within safe elastic limits.
k must keep Δx within safe range
