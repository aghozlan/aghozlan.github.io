Exercise 3: Drugs and Addiction (7 points)

Document 3: The Mechanism of Cocaine

Cocaine is a powerful nervous system stimulant drug. Under normal conditions in a healthy brain, a neurotransmitter called dopamine is released into the synaptic cleft to transmit signals associated with pleasure and reward. After the signal is transmitted, specialized "reuptake pumps" transport the dopamine back into the pre-synaptic neuron, clearing the cleft and terminating the signal.

When a person consumes cocaine, the drug molecules bind to these reuptake pumps and block them. As a result, dopamine cannot be removed from the synaptic cleft. It accumulates and continuously binds to the post-synaptic receptors, creating an amplified and prolonged feeling of euphoria. Over time, the brain adapts to this excess dopamine by reducing its own natural production and decreasing the number of active receptors. Consequently, the user requires larger and more frequent doses of the drug to achieve the same effect and to avoid severe withdrawal symptoms.

3.1.
Pick out from Document 3 the specific action of cocaine at the level of the synapse.
3.2.
Explain the consequence of this drug's action on the post-synaptic neuron.
3.3.
Document 3 states that "the user requires larger and more frequent doses of the drug to achieve the same effect". Name the scientific term that describes this physiological phenomenon.
3.4.
Drug addiction does not only alter biological functions but also impacts the individual's life. State two negative social or behavioral consequences of severe drug dependence.
