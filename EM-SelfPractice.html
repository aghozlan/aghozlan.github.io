Exercise 1: Electromagnetic Induction - From Variable Field to Moving Rod (10 points)

A rectangular conducting loop \(MNPQ\) of length \(L = 20 \text{ cm}\), width \(w = 10 \text{ cm}\), and constant electrical resistance \(R = 0.5\ \Omega\) is placed horizontally. A uniform vertical magnetic field \(\vec{B}\) passes through the loop. The positive normal \(\vec{n}\) to the surface of the loop is chosen pointing vertically upwards, parallel to the direction of \(\vec{B}\).

Part A: Variable Magnetic Field (Stationary Loop)

The loop is kept perfectly stationary. The magnitude \(B\) of the magnetic field varies with time \(t\) according to the graph shown in Figure 1.

Figure 1: Variation of the magnitude of B with respect to time
  • 1. Study of the Induced Electromotive Force
    • 1.1. Determine the expression of the magnetic flux \(\Phi(t)\) crossing the loop as a function of \(B\).
    • 1.2. Focusing on the time interval \(0 \le t \le 2\text{ s}\), calculate the rate of change of the magnetic field \(\frac{dB}{dt}\).
    • 1.3. Deduce the algebraic value of the induced electromotive force \(e\).
    • 1.4. Determine the magnitude and the direction of the induced current \(i\) in the loop. Confirm your determined direction using Lenz's law.
    • 1.5. Does an induced current exist in the loop during the interval \(2\text{ s} \le t \le 4\text{ s}\)? Justify.
    • 1.6. Compare the direction of the induced current in the interval \(4\text{ s} \le t \le 6\text{ s}\) to that in the first interval. Justify without calculation.
Part B: Constant Magnetic Field (Moving Rod)

The magnetic field is now kept constant at a magnitude of \(B = 0.5 \text{ T}\). The segment \(MN\) of the loop is replaced by a movable conducting rod of length \(L = 20 \text{ cm}\) that can slide without friction along the two parallel rails forming the rest of the loop. An external operator pulls the rod to the right with a constant velocity \(\vec{v}\) of magnitude \(v = 4 \text{ m/s}\).

Figure 2: Movable rod sliding on rails in a constant magnetic field
  • 2. Energetic Study
    • 2.1. Apply Faraday's law to calculate the induced electromotive force \(e\), then deduce the value of the induced current \(i\).
    • 2.2. The rod \(MN\) is subjected to a magnetic Laplace force \(\vec{F}_L\). Calculate its magnitude and specify its direction.
    • 2.3. To maintain the constant velocity \(\vec{v}\), an external mechanical force \(\vec{F}_{ext}\) is applied. State the relation between \(\vec{F}_{ext}\) and \(\vec{F}_L\), and determine its magnitude.
    • 2.4. Calculate the mechanical power \(P_m\) supplied by \(\vec{F}_{ext}\) and the electrical power \(P_J\) dissipated by Joule effect in the circuit. Conclude.

Exercise 1: Electromagnetic Induction (10 pts)
Q# Expected Answer / Physics Logic Pts
1.1 Area \(S = L \times w = 0.2 \times 0.1 = 0.02 \text{ m}^2\).
\(\Phi(t) = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{n} \cdot S = B(t) \cdot S \cdot \cos(0^\circ) = 0.02 \cdot B(t)\).		 1.0
1.2 From the graph, in \(0 \le t \le 2\text{ s}\), the slope is constant: \(\frac{dB}{dt} = \frac{0.5 - 0}{2 - 0} = 0.25 \text{ T/s}\). 1.0
1.3 Faraday's Law: \(e = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt} = -S \frac{dB}{dt} = -0.02 \times 0.25 = -0.005 \text{ V}\). 1.0
1.4 Magnitude: \(i = \frac{|e|}{R} = \frac{0.005}{0.5} = 0.01 \text{ A}\).
Direction: Since \(e < 0\), the current flows in the negative direction (clockwise).
Lenz justification: \(\vec{B}\) is upwards and increasing. The induced magnetic field \(\vec{B}_{ind}\) must oppose this increase by pointing downwards. By the right-hand rule, this corresponds to a clockwise current.		 2.0
1.5 No. During \(2 \le t \le 4\text{ s}\), \(B\) is constant (0.5 T). Therefore, \(\frac{dB}{dt} = 0 \implies \Phi = \text{const} \implies e = 0 \implies i = 0\). 1.0
1.6 During \(4 \le t \le 6\text{ s}\), \(B\) is decreasing. To oppose the decrease, \(\vec{B}_{ind}\) will be in the same direction as \(\vec{B}\) (upwards). This creates a counter-clockwise current, which is opposite to the direction in the first interval. 1.0
2.1 \(e = - \frac{d\Phi}{dt} = - B \cdot L \cdot v = -0.5 \times 0.2 \times 4 = -0.4 \text{ V}\).
\(i = \frac{|e|}{R} = \frac{0.4}{0.5} = 0.8 \text{ A}\).		 1.0
2.2 Laplace Force magnitude: \(F_L = i \cdot L \cdot B = 0.8 \times 0.2 \times 0.5 = 0.08 \text{ N}\).
Direction: Directed to the left (opposing the motion of the rod).		 1.0
2.3 Since velocity is constant, acceleration \(a=0\). By Newton's second law: \(\vec{F}_{ext} + \vec{F}_L = 0\). Thus, \(\vec{F}_{ext}\) is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to \(\vec{F}_L\).
\(F_{ext} = F_L = 0.08 \text{ N}\).		 0.5
2.4 Mechanical Power: \(P_m = F_{ext} \cdot v = 0.08 \times 4 = 0.32 \text{ W}\).
Joule Power: \(P_J = R \cdot i^2 = 0.5 \times (0.8)^2 = 0.32 \text{ W}\).
Conclusion: \(P_m = P_J\), showing the conservation of energy (mechanical work is entirely converted into heat).		 0.5
Exercise 2: Self-Induction (10 pts)
Q# Expected Answer / Physics Logic Pts
1.1 Drawing: Ground (COM) is connected between the resistor \(R\) and the coil. Phase \(Y_1\) is connected between the generator and the resistor \(R\). 1.0
1.2 According to Ohm's law, the voltage across the resistor is \(u_R = R \cdot i\). Thus, \(i = \frac{u_R}{R}\). Since \(R\) is a constant positive value, the graph of \(u_R(t)\) is directly proportional to \(i(t)\). 1.0
1.3 Law of addition of voltages: \(E = u_R + u_L\).
\(E = R \cdot i + r \cdot i + L \frac{di}{dt} \implies L \frac{di}{dt} + (R+r)i = E\).		 1.5
2.1 From the graph, the horizontal asymptote is at \(U_{R, max} = 8.0 \text{ V}\).
Steady-state current: \(I_0 = \frac{U_{R, max}}{R} = \frac{8}{40} = 0.2 \text{ A}\).		 1.5
2.2 In steady state, the current is constant (\(\frac{di}{dt} = 0\)). The coil behaves purely as an ohmic resistor \(r\).
\(E = (R + r) \cdot I_0 \implies 12 = (40 + r) \times 0.2 \implies 12 = 8 + 0.2r \implies 0.2r = 4 \implies r = 20\ \Omega\).		 2.0
2.3 Method: The time constant \(\tau\) is the abscissa of the point of intersection between the tangent at the origin and the horizontal asymptote representing the steady state.
Graphically, this point is at \(t = 5.0 \text{ ms} = 5 \times 10^{-3} \text{ s}\).		 1.0
2.4 \(\tau = \frac{L}{R + r} \implies L = \tau(R + r) = (5 \times 10^{-3})(40 + 20) = 5 \times 10^{-3} \times 60 = 0.3 \text{ H}\). 1.0
2.5 Maximum Magnetic Energy: \(E_m = \frac{1}{2} L I_0^2 = \frac{1}{2} (0.3)(0.2)^2 = 0.15 \times 0.04 = 0.006 \text{ J}\). 1.0
