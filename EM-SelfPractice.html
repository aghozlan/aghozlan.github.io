Exercise 1: Electromagnetic Induction - The Moving Rod (10 points)

Two parallel, horizontal conducting rails, separated by a distance \(L = 20 \text{ cm}\), are connected at one end by an ohmic resistor of resistance \(R = 4\ \Omega\). A metallic rod \(MN\) of mass \(m = 50 \text{ g}\) and negligible resistance is placed across the rails. The entire setup is immersed in a uniform, vertical magnetic field \(\vec{B}\) pointing downwards (into the page), with a magnitude \(B = 0.5 \text{ T}\).

At the instant \(t = 0\), the rod \(MN\) is at position \(x = 0\). An external mechanical agent applies a constant horizontal pulling force \(\vec{F}_{ext}\) to the right, causing the rod to slide without friction along the rails at a constant velocity \(\vec{v}\) of magnitude \(v = 4 \text{ m/s}\). The positive normal \(\vec{n}\) to the circuit is chosen parallel to and in the same direction as \(\vec{B}\).

Figure 1: Conducting rod moving uniformly on horizontal rails
Part A: Kinematics and Electrical Quantities
  • 1. Study of the Induced Current
    • 1.1. Determine the expression for the magnetic flux \(\Phi(t)\) traversing the circuit in terms of \(B\), \(L\), \(v\), and \(t\).
    • 1.2. Applying Faraday's Law, deduce the algebraic value of the induced electromotive force \(e\). Calculate its numerical value.
    • 1.3. State Lenz's Law. Based on this law, determine the physical direction of the induced current in the rod \(MN\).
    • 1.4. Calculate the magnitude \(i\) of the induced current flowing in the circuit.
Part B: Dynamic and Energetic Study
  • 2. Forces and Energy Conservation
    • 2.1. The current-carrying rod \(MN\) is subjected to a magnetic Laplace force \(\vec{F}_L\). Specify the direction of this force and calculate its magnitude.
    • 2.2. Apply Newton's Second Law to the rod \(MN\) to calculate the magnitude of the external mechanical force \(F_{ext}\).
    • 2.3. Calculate the mechanical power \(P_m\) supplied to the circuit by the external force.
    • 2.4. Calculate the electrical power \(P_J\) dissipated by the Joule effect in the resistor \(R\). Conclude on the energy transformation taking place in this setup.


Exercise 2: Self-Induction - The RL Circuit (10 points)

A circuit is set up to study the establishment of current in a coil. The circuit, shown in Figure 2, consists of the following components connected in series:

  • A DC ideal voltage generator of electromotive force \(E = 10 \text{ V}\).
  • An ohmic resistor of resistance \(R = 40\ \Omega\).
  • A coil of inductance \(L\) and internal resistance \(r\).
  • A switch \(K\).

At the instant \(t = 0\), the switch \(K\) is closed. The current \(i(t)\) flows out of the positive terminal of the generator.

Figure 2: Series RL circuit for studying the establishment of current
Part A: Graphical and Experimental Study (No Differential Equations)
  • 1. Oscilloscope Connections and Steady State

    An oscilloscope is connected to visualize the evolution of the voltage \(u_R(t)\) across the resistor \(R\) on channel \((Y_1)\). The experimental graph shows an exponential growth curve that levels off at a maximum steady-state value \(U_{R, max} = 8 \text{ V}\). The tangent at the origin intersects this asymptote at an instant \(t = 2 \text{ ms}\).

    • 1.1. Redraw Figure 2 on your answer sheet and clearly indicate the oscilloscope connections (Ground and Phase \(Y_1\)) needed to visualize the positive voltage \(u_R(t)\).
    • 1.2. Explain briefly why observing \(u_R(t)\) is experimentally equivalent to observing the variations of the current \(i(t)\).
    • 1.3. Using the maximum steady-state voltage, calculate the steady-state current \(I_0\).
    • 1.4. By applying Ohm's law for the entire circuit in the steady state, determine the internal resistance \(r\) of the coil.
    • 1.5. Using the graphical time constant \(\tau = 2 \text{ ms}\), deduce the value of the inductance \(L\).
Part B: Analytical Study (Differential Equations)
  • 2. Mathematical Modeling and Energy
    • 2.1. By applying the law of addition of voltages, derive the first-order differential equation that governs the evolution of the current \(i(t)\).
    • 2.2. Verify that \(i(t) = A (1 - e^{-t/\tau})\) is a solution to this differential equation, and find the literal expressions of \(A\) and \(\tau\) in terms of \(E\), \(R\), \(r\), and \(L\).
    • 2.3. Deduce the expression for the voltage across the coil \(u_L(t)\). Calculate the numerical value of this voltage exactly at \(t = 0\) and in the steady state (\(t \rightarrow \infty\)).
    • 2.4. Calculate the magnetic energy \(E_m\) stored in the coil when the steady state is fully established.

Exercise 1: Electromagnetic Induction (10 pts)
Q# Expected Answer / Physics Logic Pts
1.1 Surface Area: \(S = L \cdot x = L \cdot v \cdot t\).
Flux: \(\Phi = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{n} \cdot S = B \cdot L \cdot v \cdot t\).		 1.5
1.2 Faraday's Law: \(e = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt} = - B \cdot L \cdot v\).
Numerical value: \(e = -0.5 \times 0.2 \times 4 = -0.4 \text{ V}\).		 1.5
1.3 Lenz's Law definition: The induced current opposes the cause producing it.
Since \(\Phi\) is increasing, \(\vec{B}_{ind}\) is opposite to \(\vec{B}\) (upwards). By the Right-Hand Rule, current flows from \(N\) to \(M\) in the rod.		 1.0
1.4 Current magnitude: \(i = \frac{|e|}{R} = \frac{0.4}{4} = 0.1 \text{ A}\). 1.0
2.1 Direction: To the left (opposing motion via Lenz's principle).
Laplace Force: \(F_L = i \cdot L \cdot B = 0.1 \times 0.2 \times 0.5 = 0.01 \text{ N}\).		 1.5
2.2 Newton's 2nd Law: \(\sum \vec{F} = \vec{P} + \vec{N} + \vec{F}_L + \vec{F}_{ext} = m\vec{a}\).
Since \(v = \text{const}\), \(a = 0\). Projection on motion axis: \(F_{ext} - F_L = 0 \implies F_{ext} = 0.01 \text{ N}\).		 1.5
2.3 Mechanical Power: \(P_m = F_{ext} \cdot v = 0.01 \times 4 = 0.04 \text{ W}\). 1.0
2.4 Electrical Power: \(P_J = R \cdot i^2 = 4 \times (0.1)^2 = 0.04 \text{ W}\).
Conclusion: \(P_m = P_J\). Mechanical energy is completely transformed into electrical energy.		 1.0
Exercise 2: Self-Induction (10 pts)
Q# Expected Answer / Physics Logic Pts
1.1 Drawing: Ground (COM) is placed between the coil and resistor \(R\). Phase \(Y_1\) is placed on the other side of \(R\) (towards the positive terminal). 1.0
1.2 Ohm's Law across the resistor: \(u_R = R \cdot i \implies i = \frac{1}{R} u_R\). Since \(R\) is constant, the voltage curve is the exact proportional "image" of the current curve. 1.0
1.3 Steady state current: \(I_0 = \frac{U_{R, max}}{R} = \frac{8}{40} = 0.2 \text{ A}\). 1.0
1.4 In steady state, the coil acts as a pure resistor \(r\). \(E = (R + r) I_0 \implies 10 = (40 + r)(0.2) \implies r = 10\ \Omega\). 1.0
1.5 Time constant: \(\tau = \frac{L}{R_{tot}} \implies L = \tau(R + r) = (2 \times 10^{-3}) \times 50 = 0.1 \text{ H}\). 1.0
2.1 Law of addition of voltages: \(E = u_R + u_L \implies E = R \cdot i + r \cdot i + L \frac{di}{dt} \implies E = (R+r)i + L \frac{di}{dt}\). 1.5
2.2 Substitute \(i\) and \(\frac{di}{dt} = \frac{A}{\tau} e^{-t/\tau}\) into the equation:
\(E = (R+r)A - (R+r)Ae^{-t/\tau} + L \frac{A}{\tau}e^{-t/\tau}\).
By equating terms: \(A = \frac{E}{R+r}\) and \(\tau = \frac{L}{R+r}\).		 1.5
2.3 \(u_L = E - u_R = E - R \cdot i(t)\).
At \(t=0\), \(i=0 \implies u_L = 10 \text{ V}\).
At steady state, \(i=I_0 \implies u_L = 10 - 40(0.2) = 2 \text{ V}\) (or simply \(u_L = r \cdot I_0\)).		 1.0
2.4 Magnetic Energy: \(E_m = \frac{1}{2} L I_0^2 = \frac{1}{2} (0.1)(0.2)^2 = 2 \times 10^{-3} \text{ J}\). 1.0
