Exercise 1: Electromagnetic Induction - From Variable Field to Moving Rod (10 points)

A rectangular conducting loop \(MNPQ\) of constant area \(S = 0.05 \text{ m}^2\) and electrical resistance \(R = 2\ \Omega\) is placed horizontally. A uniform vertical magnetic field \(\vec{B}\) passes through the loop. The positive normal \(\vec{n}\) to the surface of the loop is chosen pointing vertically upwards, parallel to the direction of \(\vec{B}\).

Part A: Variable Magnetic Field (Stationary Loop)

The loop is kept perfectly stationary. The magnitude \(B\) of the magnetic field varies with time \(t\) according to the graph shown in Figure 1.

Figure 1: Variation of the magnitude of B with respect to time
  • 1. Study of the Induced Electromotive Force
    • 1.1. Determine the expression of the magnetic flux \(\Phi(t)\) crossing the loop as a function of \(B(t)\).
    • 1.2. Focusing on the time interval \(0 \le t \le 2\text{ s}\), calculate the rate of change of the magnetic field \(\frac{dB}{dt}\).
    • 1.3. Deduce the algebraic value of the induced electromotive force \(e\).
    • 1.4. Determine the magnitude of the induced current \(i\) in the loop.
    • 1.5. Using Lenz's law, explain the actual direction of the induced current during the interval \(0 \le t \le 2\text{ s}\).
    • 1.6. Does an induced current exist in the loop during the interval \(2\text{ s} < t \le 4\text{ s}\)? Justify.
Part B: Constant Magnetic Field (Moving Rod)

The magnetic field is now kept constant at a magnitude of \(B = 0.4 \text{ T}\). The segment \(MN\) of the loop is replaced by a movable conducting rod of length \(L = 20 \text{ cm}\) that can slide without friction along the two parallel horizontal rails. An external operator pulls the rod to the right with a constant velocity \(\vec{v}\) of magnitude \(v = 5 \text{ m/s}\).

Figure 2: Movable rod sliding on rails in a constant magnetic field
  • 2. Energetic Study
    • 2.1. Apply Faraday's law to calculate the magnitude of the induced electromotive force \(e\), then deduce the magnitude of the induced current \(i\).
    • 2.2. The rod \(MN\) is subjected to a magnetic Laplace force \(\vec{F}_L\). Calculate its magnitude.
    • 2.3. To maintain the constant velocity \(\vec{v}\), an external mechanical force \(\vec{F}_{ext}\) is applied. State the relation between \(\vec{F}_{ext}\) and \(\vec{F}_L\), and determine the magnitude of \(\vec{F}_{ext}\).
    • 2.4. Calculate the mechanical power \(P_m\) supplied by \(\vec{F}_{ext}\) and the electrical power \(P_J\) dissipated by Joule effect in the circuit. Conclude.


Exercise 2: Self-Induction - Graphical and Circuit Analysis (10 points)

To study the self-induction phenomenon of a coil of unknown inductance \(L\) and internal resistance \(r\), it is connected in series with an ideal DC voltage generator of electromotive force \(E = 12 \text{ V}\), an ohmic resistor of resistance \(R = 50\ \Omega\), and a switch \(K\) (Figure 3).

Figure 3: Series RL circuit
Part A: Phenomenon and Circuit Analysis

At the instant \(t = 0\), the switch \(K\) is closed, allowing the current \(i(t)\) to establish in the circuit.

  • 1. Analyzing the behavior of the coil
    • 1.1. Name the physical phenomenon that is responsible for the delay in the establishment of the current in the circuit.
    • 1.2. Applying the law of addition of voltages, write the relation connecting \(E\), the voltage across the resistor \(u_R\), and the voltage across the coil \(u_L\).
    • 1.3. Knowing that the current \(i = 0\) at exactly \(t = 0\), deduce the numerical value of the voltage across the coil \(u_L\) at \(t = 0\). Does the coil act as a perfect conductor or an open switch at this instant?
    • 1.4. When the steady state is reached, the current becomes constant. What does the coil act as in this steady state? Express \(u_L\) in terms of \(r\) and the steady state current \(I_0\).
Part B: Graphical Exploitation and Energy

A computerized data acquisition system acts as an oscilloscope to trace the voltage \(u_R(t)\) across the resistor. The obtained oscillogram is shown in Figure 4. The grid has a vertical sensitivity of \(S_v = 2 \text{ V/div}\) and a horizontal sensitivity of \(S_h = 1 \text{ ms/div}\).

Figure 4: Oscillogram of the voltage uR(t)
Sv = 2 V/div ; Sh = 1 ms/div
  • 2. Exploiting the experimental oscillogram
    • 2.1. Read the number of vertical divisions reached by the horizontal asymptote. Deduce the maximum steady-state voltage \(U_{R, max}\) and calculate the steady-state current \(I_0\).
    • 2.2. Utilizing the condition of the steady state found in part A, calculate the internal resistance \(r\) of the coil.
    • 2.3. Determine graphically the number of horizontal divisions representing the time constant \(\tau\) of the circuit. Explain your method and deduce the value of \(\tau\) in seconds.
    • 2.4. Knowing that the expression for the time constant is \(\tau = \frac{L}{R+r}\), calculate the precise value of the inductance \(L\).
    • 2.5. Calculate the magnetic energy \(E_m\) stored in the coil when the steady state is fully established.

Exercise 1: Electromagnetic Induction (10 pts)
Q# Expected Answer / Physics Logic Pts
1.1 \(\Phi(t) = \vec{B} \cdot \vec{n} \cdot S = B(t) \cdot S \cdot \cos(0^\circ) = 0.05 \cdot B(t)\). 1.0
1.2 From the graph, in \(0 \le t \le 2\text{ s}\), the slope is constant: \(\frac{dB}{dt} = \frac{0.4 - 0}{2 - 0} = 0.2 \text{ T/s}\). 1.0
1.3 Faraday's Law: \(e = -\frac{d\Phi}{dt} = -S \frac{dB}{dt} = -0.05 \times 0.2 = -0.01 \text{ V}\). 1.0
1.4 Magnitude: \(i = \frac{|e|}{R} = \frac{0.01}{2} = 0.005 \text{ A}\). 1.0
1.5 \(\vec{B}\) is upwards and increasing. According to Lenz's law, the induced magnetic field \(\vec{B}_{ind}\) must oppose this increase by pointing downwards. By the right-hand rule, this corresponds to a clockwise induced current. 1.5
1.6 No. During \(2 < t \le 4\text{ s}\), \(B\) is constant (0.4 T). Therefore, \(\frac{dB}{dt} = 0 \implies \Phi = \text{const} \implies e = 0 \implies i = 0\). 1.0
2.1 \(e = B \cdot L \cdot v = 0.4 \times 0.2 \times 5 = 0.4 \text{ V}\).
\(i = \frac{e}{R} = \frac{0.4}{2} = 0.2 \text{ A}\).		 1.0
2.2 Laplace Force magnitude: \(F_L = i \cdot L \cdot B = 0.2 \times 0.2 \times 0.4 = 0.016 \text{ N}\). 1.0
2.3 Since velocity is constant, acceleration \(a=0\). By Newton's second law: \(\vec{F}_{ext} + \vec{F}_L = \vec{0}\). Thus, \(\vec{F}_{ext}\) is equal in magnitude and opposite in direction to \(\vec{F}_L\).
\(F_{ext} = F_L = 0.016 \text{ N}\).		 1.0
2.4 Mechanical Power: \(P_m = F_{ext} \cdot v = 0.016 \times 5 = 0.08 \text{ W}\).
Joule Power: \(P_J = R \cdot i^2 = 2 \times (0.2)^2 = 0.08 \text{ W}\).
Conclusion: \(P_m = P_J\), showing the conservation of energy.		 0.5
Exercise 2: Self-Induction (10 pts)
Q# Expected Answer / Physics Logic Pts
1.1 The physical phenomenon is Self-Induction. 1.0
1.2 Law of addition of voltages: \(E = u_R + u_L\). 1.0
1.3 At \(t = 0\), \(i = 0\), so according to Ohm's law \(u_R = R \cdot i = 0\).
Substituting in the addition of voltages: \(E = 0 + u_L \implies u_L = E = 12 \text{ V}\).
Because it takes the full voltage while allowing no current, it acts as an open switch.		 2.0
1.4 In the steady state, \(\frac{di}{dt} = 0\), so the coil acts purely as an ohmic resistor \(r\).
Therefore, \(u_L = r \cdot I_0\).		 1.5
2.1 From the oscillogram, the horizontal asymptote levels off at 5 vertical divisions.
\(U_{R, max} = 5 \text{ div} \times 2 \text{ V/div} = 10.0 \text{ V}\).
Steady-state current: \(I_0 = \frac{U_{R, max}}{R} = \frac{10}{50} = 0.2 \text{ A}\).		 1.5
2.2 In steady state, \(E = u_{R, max} + u_L = U_{R, max} + r \cdot I_0\).
\(12 = 10 + r(0.2) \implies r(0.2) = 2 \implies r = 10\ \Omega\).		 1.0
2.3 Method: The point of intersection between the tangent at the origin \((T)\) and the horizontal asymptote occurs at exactly 2 horizontal divisions.
\(\tau = 2 \text{ div} \times 1 \text{ ms/div} = 2.0 \text{ ms} = 2 \times 10^{-3} \text{ s}\).		 1.0
2.4 \(\tau = \frac{L}{R + r} \implies L = \tau(R + r) = (2 \times 10^{-3})(50 + 10) = 2 \times 10^{-3} \times 60 = 0.12 \text{ H}\). 0.5
2.5 Maximum Magnetic Energy: \(E_m = \frac{1}{2} L I_0^2 = \frac{1}{2} (0.12)(0.2)^2 = 0.06 \times 0.04 = 0.0024 \text{ J}\). 0.5
