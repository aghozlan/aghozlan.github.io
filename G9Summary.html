Forces, Equilibrium, Principle of Interaction, & Hooke's Law

1. Forces & Their Characteristics

Force: A mechanical action capable of creating motion, modifying motion, or deforming an object. It is represented by a vector →F.

4 Characteristics of a Force

  • 1. Point of Application: Where the force acts.
  • 2. Line of Action: Vertical, horizontal, or oblique.
  • 3. Direction: Up, down, left, right, etc.
  • 4. Magnitude: The numerical value measured by a spring balance (Unit: Newton, N).

Drawing Rules (Point of Application)

Contact Forces

Drawn starting from the exact point of contact between the object and the actor.

Forces at a Distance

Drawn starting from the center of gravity (center of the object).

Specific Forces & How to Represent Them

Weight (→W) DISTANCE

Starts from center.
Vertical, downward.

Tension (→T) CONTACT

Starts at contact point.
Along rope, outward.

Normal (→N) CONTACT

Starts at contact surface.
Perpendicular, outward.

Friction (→f) CONTACT

Starts at contact surface.
Parallel, opposes motion.

Buoyant (→FB) CONTACT

Starts at center of immersed part. Upward.

Air Resist. (→f) CONTACT

From surface exposed to air. Opposes motion.

Magnetic / Elec. DISTANCE

Starts from the center.
Points towards/away.

Muscular (→F) CONTACT

Starts at exact point where hand/muscle pushes.

Focus: Weight of a Body (→W)

  • Point of application: Center of gravity (G).
  • Line of action: Vertical.
  • Direction: Downwards.
W = m × g
W : Weight in Newtons (N) m : Mass in kilograms (kg) g : Gravitational strength (N/kg)

2. Equilibrium & Interaction

Equilibrium of a Body Subjected to 2 Forces

When a body is at rest (equilibrium) under the action of two forces →F1 and →F2, these forces cancel each other out.

Vector Relation:

→F1 + →F2 = →0  →  →F1 = –→F2

Conclusion: The 2 forces have:

  • Same line of action
  • Opposite directions
  • Same magnitude: F1 = F2

Principle of Interaction

When body A exerts a force on body B, body B simultaneously exerts a force on body A.

→FA/B = –→FB/A

Magnitudes are equal:
FA/B = FB/A

3. Hooke's Law

Within the elastic limit, the tension force of a spring is directly proportional to its deformation (elongation or compression).

F = k × x
FForce(Newtons, N)
kStiffness(N/m)
xDeformation(meters, m)
Elongated Spring (x > 0)
Compressed Spring (x < 0)

Graphical Representation

The curve is a straight line passing through the origin, showing direct proportionality.

Stiffness (k) = Slope
k = ΔF / Δx = (F2 - F1) / (x2 - x1)

• Elastic Limit: Beyond this point, the spring is permanently deformed and Hooke's Law fails.
