Light Travels in Straight Lines & Shadows
PREREQUISITE
💡 Rectilinear Propagation of Light

Light energy travels outward from its source in straight lines called rays. This property is called rectilinear propagation. It explains why shadows have crisp edges and why a small hole can project an inverted image like a pinhole camera.

Key consequence: Because light cannot bend around objects under normal conditions, opaque objects block light and cast shadows — regions where light cannot reach.

Explain — The Laws of Reflection
EXPLAIN
🔮 Secret Physics: Use the UV Torch to Reveal

The laws of reflection have been known since ancient times. Shine the UV torch to unveil them!

LAW 1 — Angle of Incidence (i) = Angle of Reflection (r)
LAW 2 — The incident ray, normal, and reflected ray all lie in the same plane
LAW 3 — All angles are measured from the NORMAL (not from the mirror surface)
🎯 Objective 1 — Regular Reflection vs. Diffuse Scattering
Regular (Specular) Reflection: Occurs on smooth, polished surfaces. All reflected rays are parallel → a clear image is formed. Examples: mirror, still water, polished metal.
🎯 Objective 2 — Conditions for Regular Reflection
1️⃣
Light must reach the surface
An incident ray must strike the reflecting surface.
2️⃣
Surface must be opaque
Transparent objects allow light to pass through rather than reflecting it.
3️⃣
Surface must be smooth (polished)
Rough surfaces produce diffuse scattering instead.
🎯 Objective 3 — Constructing a Ray Diagram Step by Step
📏
Step 1: Draw the mirror as a straight horizontal line.
📐
Step 2: At the point of incidence, draw the NORMAL — a dashed line perpendicular (90°) to the mirror.
↗️
Step 3: Use your protractor to measure angle i between the incident ray and the normal.
✅
Step 4: On the other side of the normal, draw the reflected ray at the same angle: r = i. Add an arrow.
i = r  •  Angle of Incidence = Angle of Reflection
