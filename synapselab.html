E3 · Structure

Anatomy of a Synapse

A synapse is a junction between two neurones. It is not a physical connection — it is a precisely engineered chemical relay station.

Structure 01
Presynaptic Neurone
The neurone sending the message. Contains the synaptic knob at its terminal end. Carries the electrical action potential toward the synapse.
Structure 02
Synaptic Knob
Swollen terminal of the presynaptic neurone. Contains mitochondria (for energy) and synaptic vesicles packed with neurotransmitters.
Structure 03
Synaptic Vesicles
Tiny membrane-bound sacs inside the synaptic knob. Each vesicle stores thousands of neurotransmitter molecules ready for release.
Structure 04
Synaptic Cleft
The 20 nanometre gap between the two neurones. Too wide for an electrical signal to cross — neurotransmitters must diffuse across it.
Structure 05
Postsynaptic Membrane
The membrane of the receiving neurone. Contains specific receptor proteins that bind neurotransmitters — like a lock waiting for its key.
Structure 06
Receptor Proteins
Complementary-shaped proteins on the postsynaptic membrane. Only the correct neurotransmitter fits — ensuring signal specificity (lock and key).
Structure 07
Mitochondria
Present in large numbers in the synaptic knob. Provide ATP energy for moving vesicles to the membrane and for active reuptake of neurotransmitters.
Key Fact
One-way only
Synapses are unidirectional — signals can only travel from pre → post synaptic neurone. Receptors exist only on the postsynaptic side.
💡 Answer to the opening question:

The electrical impulse cannot cross the synaptic cleft. Instead, it triggers the release of neurotransmitters from synaptic vesicles. These chemical molecules diffuse across the 20nm gap and bind to receptor proteins on the postsynaptic membrane, generating a new electrical impulse in the next neurone.

E3 · Transmission

How the Signal Crosses

Seven steps convert an electrical impulse into a chemical signal and back again. Open the 3D model alongside this page to see each step in full detail.

🌐 Watch each step animate in real-time
1
Action Potential Arrives
An electrical action potential travels along the presynaptic neurone and reaches the synaptic knob. The electrical signal cannot cross the cleft on its own.
2
Ca²⁺ Channels Open
The action potential causes calcium ion channels to open. Ca²⁺ floods into the synaptic knob — this is the essential trigger for vesicle release.
3
Vesicles Fuse (Exocytosis)
Ca²⁺ causes synaptic vesicles to move to and fuse with the presynaptic membrane — a process called exocytosis.
4
Neurotransmitters Released
Thousands of neurotransmitter molecules pour into the 20nm synaptic cleft and diffuse toward the postsynaptic membrane.
5
Neurotransmitters Diffuse
Molecules diffuse across the cleft by concentration gradient — from high concentration (cleft) to low (postsynaptic membrane).
6
Receptor Binding → New Impulse
Neurotransmitters bind to complementary receptor proteins on the postsynaptic membrane, opening ion channels and generating a new action potential.
7
Neurotransmitter Removal
Neurotransmitters are either broken down by enzymes (e.g. acetylcholinesterase) or reabsorbed by reuptake into the presynaptic neurone. This resets the synapse for the next signal.
E3 · Neurotransmitters

The Chemical Messengers

Neurotransmitters are the chemical molecules that carry signals across the synaptic cleft. Different neurotransmitters have different effects — some excite the next neurone, some inhibit it.

Acetylcholine (ACh)
Case Study · Most studied
Released at neuromuscular junctions — where nerves meet muscles. Causes muscle contraction. Also active in memory and attention.

After binding, it is broken down by the enzyme acetylcholinesterase to stop the signal.
Excitatory
Dopamine
Reward & Motivation
Released in the brain's reward pathway. Associated with pleasure, motivation, and movement coordination.

Low dopamine is linked to Parkinson's disease. Some drugs hijack this pathway.
Excitatory/Inhibitory
Serotonin
Mood & Wellbeing
Regulates mood, sleep, and appetite. Low serotonin is associated with depression.

Antidepressants called SSRIs work by blocking serotonin reuptake — keeping more in the cleft longer.
Mostly inhibitory
Adrenaline
Fight or Flight
Released during stress or danger. Increases heart rate, dilates pupils, redirects blood to muscles.

Prepares the body for immediate action — the classic fight-or-flight response.
Excitatory
GABA
The Brain's Brake
The main inhibitory neurotransmitter in the brain. Reduces neuronal excitability — calms the nervous system.

Anti-anxiety medications work by enhancing GABA activity.
Inhibitory
Reuptake
Switching off the signal
After binding receptors, neurotransmitters must be removed to end the signal. Two methods: enzymatic breakdown (ACh → acetylcholinesterase) or reuptake (pumped back into the presynaptic neurone for reuse).
Signal termination
🔬 ACh Case Study — Why It Matters:

Acetylcholine is the neurotransmitter at every neuromuscular junction in your body. The poison curare (used on arrow tips) blocks ACh receptors → muscles cannot contract → paralysis. The nerve agent sarin blocks acetylcholinesterase → ACh stays in the cleft → muscles in constant contraction. Understanding synapses saves lives.
