1.75 ptsQ2. ICE table + Kc + yield

n_eq1−x3−xxx
2b (1.5 pt):
K_eq = x² / [(1−x)(3−x)] = 4
→ x² = 4(3 − 4x + x²) → 3x² − 16x + 12 = 0
Discriminant = 256 − 144 = 112  →  √112 ≈ 10.58
x = (16 − 10.58)/6 ≈ 0.90 mol  (reject x = 4.43, exceeds limiting reagent) ✓
2c (0.5 pt): Yield = 0.90/1 × 100 = 90 % (limiting reagent = acid = 1 mol)
0.75 ptQ3. Compare yields — Le Chatelier
66.7 % (1:1) < 90 % (1:3). Excess alcohol (3 mol vs 1 mol) shifts the equilibrium to the right (Le Chatelier's principle: increasing a reactant's concentration favours the forward reaction), producing more ester and increasing the yield.
1 ptQ4. Reaction quotient and direction
Q_c = n(ester)×n(water) / [n(acid)×n(alcohol)] = (0.3 × 0.3)/(1 × 1) = 0.09
Qc = 0.09 < Keq = 4 → Forward direction (more ester and water will be produced until equilibrium is reached).
0.75 ptQ5. Two characteristics of esterification
  • (i) Reversible (equilibrium) reaction — the reaction does not go to completion; it reaches a state of dynamic equilibrium characterised by Keq.
  • (ii) Slow reaction — even with a catalyst it requires several hours to reach equilibrium; without a catalyst it is extremely slow at room temperature.
Also accepted as second characteristic: athermal / ΔH ≈ 0 (the equilibrium position does not change significantly with temperature).

0.5 ptQ1.1. Molar concentration of Si
Mass percentage: P = m(HCl)/m(solution) × 100 = Ci×M(HCl)/(d×1000) × 100
⇒ Ci = P×d×1000 / (M(HCl)×100) = 38×1.2×1000 / (36.5×100) = 12.5 mol·L−1
0.5 ptQ1.2. Volume Vi to withdraw
Conservation of moles: Ci×Vi = Cf×Vf
Vi = Cf×Vf / Ci = 0.1×250 / 12.5 = 2 mL
0.5 ptQ1.3. Glassware choice
Volumetric pipette 2 mL (to withdraw Vi = 2 mL precisely) + Volumetric flask 250 mL (to prepare Sf).
0.75 ptQ2.1. Moles of each reactant
n0(HCl) = Cf×V = 0.1×150×10−3 = 15×10−3 mol (0.25 pt)
n0(CaCO3) = m/M = 500×10−3/100 = 5×10−3 mol (0.5 pt)
0.75 ptQ2.2. Limiting reactant
Stoichiometric ratio (S.R.):
R(HCl) = n0(HCl)/2 = 15×10−3/2 = 7.5×10−3
R(CaCO3) = n0(CaCO3)/1 = 5×10−3
R(HCl) > R(CaCO3) ⇒ CaCO3 is the limiting reactant.
0.75 ptQ2.3. Is t = 60 min the end of the reaction?
From stoichiometry (S.R. based on limiting reactant CaCO3):
n∞(CO2) = n0(CaCO3)/1 = 5×10−3 mol
At t = 60 min: n60(CO2) = 4×10−3 mol < 5×10−3 mol
⇒ t = 60 min does NOT correspond to the end of the reaction. (0.25 pt each conclusion)
1 ptQ2.4. Curve n(CO2) = f(t) with annotations
Award: correct axes (0.25 pt) + correct data points (0.5 pt) + smooth asymptotic shape (0.25 pt).
1 ptQ2.5. Evolution of instantaneous rate
The instantaneous rate at time t is proportional to the slope of the tangent to the curve at t.
As time progresses, the curve becomes less steep (the tangent slope decreases).
⇒ The instantaneous rate of the reaction decreases over time.
Justification: concentrations of reactants decrease as the reaction proceeds, so the rate decreases (kinetic factor: concentration).
1 ptQ2.6. Half-life time t½
The half-life is the time for the limiting reactant (CaCO3) to decrease to half its initial amount, equivalent to producing half the maximum CO2:
n∞(CO2)/2 = 5×10−3/2 = 2.5×10−3 mol
Draw a horizontal line at n = 2.5×10−3 mol until it meets the curve, then drop a vertical to the time axis.
t½ ≈ 19 min (read from graph — accept 18–21 min).
0.25 ptQ2.7. Reaction rate relationship
The balanced equation shows 2 mol HCl consumed per mol CaCO3 (and per mol CO2).
Rate of disappearance of HCl = 2 × rate of formation of CO2 = 2 × r(rxn).
Answer: b) r(HCl) = 2 × r(rxn)

0.5 ptQ1.1. Molecular formula of (A)
General formula of saturated monoalcohol: CnH2n+2O
%C = 12n/(12n+2n+2+16) × 100 = 12n/(14n+18) × 100 = 60
12n = 0.60(14n+18) = 8.4n+10.8 → 3.6n = 10.8 → n = 3
Molecular formula: C₃H₈O ✓
0.5 ptQ1.2. Two alcohol isomers
  • Propan-1-ol: CH₃−CH₂−CH₂−OH  (primary alcohol)
  • Propan-2-ol: CH₃−CH(OH)−CH₃  (secondary alcohol)
0.25 ptQ2.1. Chemical family of (Y)
Y is positive to 2,4-DNPH (has C=O) AND positive to Tollens' → Y is an aldehyde (functional group: −CHO).
0.75 ptQ2.2. Match X, Y, Z
  • X = Propanone (CH₃−CO−CH₃): (+) 2,4-DNPH (has C=O) but (−) Tollens' (not an aldehyde, it's a ketone) → ketone.
  • Y = Propanal (CH₃−CH₂−CHO): (+) both tests → aldehyde.
  • Z = Propanoic acid (CH₃−CH₂−COOH): (−) both carbonyl tests (−COOH ≠ C=O in ketone/aldehyde sense for 2,4-DNPH under these conditions); releases CO₂ with NaHCO₃ → carboxylic acid.
0.5 ptQ2.3. Isomer giving compound X
X = propanone (a ketone). Ketones are formed by mild oxidation of secondary alcohols. Since propan-2-ol is the secondary isomer of C₃H₈O, it is propan-2-ol that gives X (propanone), as a single product regardless of oxidant quantity.
0.5 ptQ3.1. Name and characteristics of reaction R'
Name: Esterification
Kinetic characteristic: slow reaction (requires acid catalyst and heating)
Thermodynamic characteristic: reversible (equilibrium) reaction / athermal (ΔH ≈ 0, equilibrium position barely changes with temperature)
0.75 ptQ3.2. Show K_c = α²/(1−α)² and calculate α
n₀(acid)=n₀(alcohol)=0.6 mol; at equilibrium: n(ester)=n(water)=0.6α; n(acid)=n(alcohol)=0.6(1−α)
K_c = (0.6α)²/[0.6(1−α)]² = α²/(1−α)² ✓
K_c = 4 → α/(1−α)=2 → α=2−2α → 3α=2 → α = 2/3 ≈ 0.667 (66.7 %)
0.25 ptQ3.3. Effect on yield
3.3a: Adding a catalyst: No change in yield α. The catalyst speeds up both forward and reverse reactions equally; it does not change the equilibrium position.
3.3b: Continuously removing water: Yield α increases. Removing a product shifts the equilibrium to the right (Le Chatelier's principle), producing more ester.
