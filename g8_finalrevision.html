Chapter 1 — Mechanical Waves

Waves — Properties & Calculations

Answer 3 MCQs, then complete the numeric exercises. (5 pts)

Q1 1 pt — A wave has frequency f = 4 Hz and wavelength λ = 0.5 m. What is its wave speed?
✓ Correct! v = f × λ = 4 Hz × 0.5 m = 2 m/s. Always multiply frequency by wavelength.
🟡 Hint 1: The formula connecting wave speed, frequency and wavelength is v = f × λ
🟣 Hint 2: f = 4 Hz means 4 waves per second; λ = 0.5 m is the length of each wave
🔵 Hint 3: v = 4 × 0.5 = 2 m/s
Q2 1 pt — A transverse wave is one where the particles vibrate…
✓ Transverse waves: particles oscillate perpendicular to energy flow. Example: light, water surface waves.
🟡 Hint 1: Compare longitudinal (particles ∥ direction) vs transverse (particles ⊥ direction)
🟣 Hint 2: Sound is longitudinal. Light is transverse.
🔵 Hint 3: "Perpendicular" = at right angles = 90°
Q3 1 pt — The period T of a wave with frequency f = 2 Hz is…
✓ T = 1/f = 1/2 = 0.5 s. Period and frequency are reciprocals.
🔢 Numeric Exercise 2 pts

Calculate: (a) its frequency f, and (b) its period T.
f = v ÷ λ = ? (Hz)

Q1 1 pt — All EM waves travel in a vacuum at the speed of light. What is this speed?
✓ All EM waves travel at c = 3 × 10⁸ m/s in a vacuum. This is a key constant to memorise!
Q2 1 pt — Which EM wave is used by doctors to see bones inside the human body?
✓ X-rays penetrate soft tissue but are absorbed by dense bone — producing medical images.
Q3 1 pt — An EM wave has frequency f = 6 × 10¹⁴ Hz. Using v = 3 × 10⁸ m/s, its wavelength λ is…
✓ λ = v/f = (3×10⁸) ÷ (6×10¹⁴) = 5 × 10⁻⁷ m — visible light (green).
🟡 Hint 1: Rearrange v = f × λ → λ = v ÷ f
🟣 Hint 2: (3 × 10⁸) ÷ (6 × 10¹⁴) = (3/6) × 10⁸⁻¹⁴ = 0.5 × 10⁻⁶
🔵 Hint 3: 0.5 × 10⁻⁶ = 5 × 10⁻⁷ m

Chapter 3 — Rectilinear Propagation & Shadow

Light Sources, Shadows & Propagation

Study the diagram, then answer 3 questions + journal. (4 pts)

🖼 Shadow Diagram — Study carefully
A = Point light source  ·  B = Opaque object  ·  C = Screen  ·  Dashed lines = light rays
Q1 1 pt — In the diagram, what is the dark region on the screen called?
✓ The umbra is the region of total darkness — no light reaches it at all from the source.
Q2 1 pt — The diagram shows a point source lamp. Why is there NO penumbra (partial shadow) in this diagram?
✓ A point source emits light from one exact spot. With no width, every ray either hits the object or misses it — giving a perfect sharp umbra and no penumbra.
🟡 Hint 1: Penumbra requires some rays to reach a spot from PART of the source — possible only if source has SIZE
🟣 Hint 2: Point source = zero size → either a ray passes or it doesn't → only umbra
🔵 Hint 3: Extended source (like the Sun) creates both umbra AND penumbra
Q3 1 pt — Light travels in straight lines (rectilinear propagation). This is shown in the diagram by…
✓ The straight rays in the diagram directly model rectilinear propagation — light never curves in a uniform medium.

Chapter 4 — Reflection of Light

Law of Reflection — Interactive Protractor

Use the protractor, then answer 3 questions. (5 pts)

🎯 Interactive Protractor Exercise 1 pt
The incident ray hits the mirror at 40° from the normal. Click ABOVE the mirror on the RIGHT side to draw your reflected ray. Then click Check.
🟡 Hint: The law of reflection says: angle of incidence = angle of reflection, both measured from the normal (dashed line)
🟣 Hint: The incident ray is on the LEFT of the normal. Your reflected ray goes on the RIGHT at the SAME angle
Q1 1 pt — The law of reflection states that the angle of incidence…
✓ i = r. Both angles are measured from the normal (perpendicular to the mirror surface).
Q2 1 pt — A ray hits a mirror at 65° to the mirror surface. What is the angle of reflection (measured from the normal)?
✓ Angle to surface = 65° → Angle to normal = 90° − 65° = 25°. Angle of reflection = 25°.
🟡 Hint 1: Angles of incidence and reflection are ALWAYS measured from the normal, not from the mirror surface
🟣 Hint 2: Angle from surface + angle from normal = 90°
🔵 Hint 3: 90° − 65° = 25° (angle from normal) → reflected at 25°
Q3 1 pt — In reflection, the incident ray, reflected ray and normal all lie in…
✓ All three lines lie in the same plane — this is the second law of reflection.
