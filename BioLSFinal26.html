Exercise 3: Immune Responses and Diagnostic Methods (5 points)

Hepatitis B Virus (HBV) is an intracellular pathogen that infects liver cells (hepatocytes). The eradication of this virus involves complex cellular collaborations. To study these mechanisms, scientists extract macrophages and lymphocytes from an individual who recently recovered from an HBV infection.

Part A: Cellular Collaboration and Cytotoxicity

The extracted macrophages are cultured in vitro with the HBV virus. After a few hours, specific CD8+ T Lymphocytes (extracted from the same individual) are added to the culture. The sequence of cellular interactions is visualized in Document 1.

Document 1: Sequential cellular interactions during the immune response to HBV

Following this activation, the T8 lymphocytes differentiate into Effector Cytotoxic T Lymphocytes (CTLs). To prove the specificity of these CTLs, a Chromium-51 release assay is performed. Three lots of target cells are loaded with radioactive Chromium ($^{51}$Cr). When a target cell is destroyed (lysed), it releases $^{51}$Cr into the medium. The results are shown in Document 2.

Lot Target Cells Effector Cells Added Lysis ($^{51}$Cr Release)
A Patient's Hepatocytes + HBV Virus Patient's CTLs 88 % (Massive)
B Patient's Hepatocytes + HCV (Hepatitis C Virus) Patient's CTLs 2 % (Negligible)
C Different Individual's Hepatocytes + HBV Virus Patient's CTLs 3 % (Negligible)

Document 2: In vitro cytotoxicity assay using Chromium-51 release

Part B: Serum Diagnosis

To verify if a new patient is currently infected with HBV, an Ouchterlony immunodiffusion test is performed. Soluble HBV antigens are placed in the central well (C). Serum from a known healthy individual is placed in Well 1, and the new patient's serum is placed in Well 2. Document 3 shows the result after 48 hours.

Document 3: Ouchterlony immunodiffusion gel after 48 hours

QDetailed Answer Key & Logical StepsPts
1 Normal parents I-1 and I-2 have an affected son, II-1. This means that both parents carried the disease allele but it was masked by the normal trait. The disease skipped a generation. Therefore, the allele responsible for Alport Syndrome is Recessive. 1
2 Analysis: Individual I-1 is normal and has only one band, $A_1$. Individual II-1 is affected and has only one band, $A_2$. Individual I-2 is normal but has both bands ($A_1$ and $A_2$), proving she is a heterozygous carrier.
Matching: Allele $A_1$ corresponds to the Normal phenotype (dominant, let's call it $N$). Allele $A_2$ corresponds to the Affected phenotype (recessive, let's call it $d$). 		1
3 Hypothesis 1: Autosomal. If autosomal, all individuals must possess two alleles (two bands or a double-intensity single band). I-1 is a normal male. If autosomal, his genotype is $N/n$ or $N/N$. Since he has an affected son II-1 ($d/d$), I-1 must be heterozygous $N/d$ and should show TWO bands on the gel. However, Document 2 shows I-1 has ONLY ONE band ($A_1$). Thus, the autosomal hypothesis is rejected.
Hypothesis 2: Sex-Linked (X chromosome). If X-linked, males ($XY$) have only one allele. Normal male I-1 is $X^N Y$, which perfectly explains his single $A_1$ band. Affected male II-1 is $X^d Y$, explaining his single $A_2$ band. Carrier female I-2 is $X^N X^d$, explaining her two bands ($A_1$ and $A_2$). Carrier female II-3 is also $X^N X^d$ (two bands). The Y chromosome carries no allele, which matches the electrophoresis data perfectly.
Conclusion: The gene is located on the X sex chromosome. 		2
4 Sex: Male.
Phenotype: Affected with Alport Syndrome.
Justification: The fetus has ONLY the $A_2$ band. Since the mother II-2 is normal, she could be a carrier ($X^N X^d$) and pass the $X^d$ allele to the fetus. If the fetus were female, she would receive a normal $X^N$ from her normal father (II-5, $X^N Y$), resulting in an $X^N X^d$ genotype and TWO bands. Because the fetus has only ONE band ($A_2$), it must have received a Y chromosome from the father. Thus, the genotype is $X^d Y$ (Male, Affected). 		1
