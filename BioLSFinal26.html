Exercise 1: Hormonal Control of Spermatogenesis (4 points)

The production of male gametes, spermatogenesis, is a continuous process occurring in the seminiferous tubules of the testes. It is controlled by the hypothalamic-pituitary complex. To understand this regulation, experiments were conducted on adult male mammals. Document 1 summarizes the experiments and their corresponding results.

Experiments Results on the Testes
1. Hypophysectomy (Ablation of the anterior pituitary gland) Cessation of spermatogenesis; atrophy of Leydig cells; drop in testosterone level to near zero.
2. Hypophysectomy + Continuous injection of physiological doses of FSH Resumption of spermatogenesis; Leydig cells remain atrophied; testosterone level remains near zero.
3. Hypophysectomy + Continuous injection of physiological doses of LH Restoration of normal Leydig cell mass and testosterone secretion; no resumption of spermatogenesis initially.
4. Hypophysectomy + Injection of LH and FSH Complete restoration of normal testicular functions (spermatogenesis and testosterone secretion).

Document 1: Experimental interventions and results

  1. Analyze the results of experiments 1, 2, and 3. What can you deduce regarding the target cells of FSH and LH? (1.5 pts)
  2. Specify the exact cells within the seminiferous tubules that are stimulated by FSH to support spermatogenesis. (1 pt)
  3. Based on your acquired knowledge and Document 1, explain how LH indirectly ensures the completion of spermatogenesis. (1.5 pts)

QAnswer Key & JustificationsPts
1 Analysis: Ablation of the anterior pituitary (Exp 1) results in cessation of spermatogenesis and atrophy of Leydig cells, showing pituitary control. Injection of FSH alone (Exp 2) restores spermatogenesis but Leydig cells remain atrophied. Injection of LH alone (Exp 3) restores Leydig cell mass and testosterone secretion, but not spermatogenesis.
Deduction: The target cells for FSH are the cells responsible for spermatogenesis (Sertoli/germ cells) inside the seminiferous tubules, while the target cells for LH are the interstitial Leydig cells located between the tubules. 		1.5
2 The specific cells inside the seminiferous tubules targeted by FSH are the Sertoli cells. Their role is to support, protect, and nourish the germ cells, and secrete androgen-binding protein (ABP) which concentrates testosterone in the tubules. 1
3 Based on Document 1 (Exp 3 & 4), LH stimulates the Leydig cells to secrete testosterone. This testosterone is essential because, when combined with the effects of FSH, it allows the complete restoration of testicular function. Thus, LH indirectly stimulates spermatogenesis by triggering the secretion of testosterone, which is the direct hormonal stimulator of the final stages of sperm production. 1.5

Exercise 2: The Female Sexual Cycle (5 pts)

QAnswer Key & JustificationsPts
1 Follicular phase: Day 1 to Day 14. Justification: Document 2 shows the presence of estrogen only (increasing to a peak at day 13), secreted by the developing follicles, while progesterone is almost zero.
Luteal phase: Day 14 to Day 28. Justification: Document 2 shows the secretion of both estrogen and progesterone (peaking at day 21), characteristic of the corpus luteum activity. 		1.5
2 During the follicular phase leading up to day 13, the follicles grow and mature into a dominant Graafian follicle. As the follicle increases in size and the number of follicular cells multiplies, it secretes increasing amounts of estrogen, which explains the peak observed on day 13. 1
3 The high peak of estrogen on day 13 exerts a positive feedback on the hypothalamic-pituitary complex, triggering a massive surge of LH (and a smaller surge of FSH). The immediate consequence on the ovary on day 14 is ovulation (rupture of the follicle and release of the oocyte). 1.5
4 Continuous high doses of synthetic estrogen and progesterone exert a strong negative feedback on the hypothalamus and pituitary gland. This inhibits the secretion of GnRH, FSH, and LH. Without the LH surge at mid-cycle, ovulation cannot occur, preventing pregnancy. 1

QAnswer Key & JustificationsPts
1 The role played is an Antigen Presenting Cell (APC). The specific surface molecule used to present the viral antigen to the lymphocyte is the HLA (Human Leukocyte Antigen) / MHC (Major Histocompatibility Complex). 1

Differentiation into effector Cytotoxic T Lymphocytes (CTLs) and memory T cells. 3. Effector phase: CTLs recognize infected cells displaying the viral antigen and destroy them by releasing perforin and granzymes (cytolysis / apoptosis). 1.5

Exercise 4: Transmission of a Hereditary Trait (5 pts)

QAnswer Key & JustificationsPts
1 The allele is Dominant.
Justification: The affected mother I-2 has an affected daughter II-2 and an affected son II-4. Also, unaffected parents (II-1 and II-5) have only unaffected children (III-1). If the disease was recessive, affected individuals would need to receive the affected allele from both parents, but I-1 is normal. Thus, the normal trait is recessive, and the disease trait is dominant. 		1.5
2 Hypothesis 1: X-linked dominant. If the gene is carried by the X chromosome, an affected father must pass his affected X chromosome to ALL his daughters. However, in this pedigree, we don't have an affected father mating to test this directly. Let's look at a normal mother. A normal mother (recessive, $X^n X^n$) and normal father ($X^n Y$) will have 100% normal children. This matches II-1, II-5, and III-1.
Furthermore, affected female I-2 ($X^N X^n$) and normal male I-1 ($X^n Y$) can have normal sons (II-1), normal daughters (II-3), affected daughters (II-2), and affected sons (II-4). This perfectly matches the pedigree.
Hypothesis 2: Y-linked. Rejected, because females (I-2, II-2) are affected.
Hypothesis 3: Autosomal dominant. If autosomal dominant, I-2 is Nn and I-1 is nn. They can have normal (nn) and affected (Nn) offspring of both sexes. This also fits perfectly.
*Note:* The pedigree actually fits both Autosomal Dominant and X-linked Dominant. However, Hypophosphatemia is a classic X-linked dominant disorder. Based strictly on the pedigree, both hypotheses are valid. Full marks given for correctly testing and accepting Autosomal Dominant OR X-linked Dominant with proper justification. 		2
3 Let N be the dominant disease allele and n be the normal recessive allele.
- Genotype of I-1: nn (or $X^n Y$ if X-linked) because he is phenotypically normal (recessive).
- Genotype of I-2: Nn (or $X^N X^n$ if X-linked) because she is affected but has normal children (recessive n from her to II-1 and II-3).
- Genotype of II-3: nn (or $X^n X^n$ if X-linked) because she is phenotypically normal. 		1.5
